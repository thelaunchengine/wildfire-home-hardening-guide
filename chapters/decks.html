Surfaces of decks are either solid or constructed using deck boards. Solid surface decks are commonly lightweight concrete or tile. Noncombustible deck board types typically include metal and fiber cement. Solid wood and wood-plastic composites (which can melt) are combustible deck board types.

Raised decks should have a noncombustible area underneath them, such as gravel. They may also be enclosed to reduce the potential for ember entry.

On this page, Santa Barbara County Fire Safe Council recommends alterations that can be made to your structure to reduce its vulnerability to wildfire. At this time, we do not recommend or provide links to specific products or contractors.

The recommended alterations are ordered from least expensive to most expensive.

Where deck, porch, railings, or balcony structures intersect with exterior walls, noncombustible flashing can be installed between the underside of the exterior siding and the top surface of the deck.

Enclose your deck if it is open underneath to avoid places where embers and flames can enter and ignite it. Multi-story decks are often hard if not impossible to enclose. If this is the case, please refer to the Deck Maintenance tab for how to keep this area fire-resistant.

Keep in mind that decks greater than four feet in height must be accessible from below and be maintained to ensure the area underneath is clear of debris. Regardless, if your deck is made of planks, it is good to be able to access the area underneath your deck to clean out debris and leaf litter that will accumulate.

A more cost-effective fire-safe upgrade for combustible decks is to replace the first 5 feet of the deck nearest the home, including the railings, with noncombustible material such as metal, tile, cement, or Hardie board. Most wood-plastic composites, high-density tropical hardwood, and fire retardant treated wood are less vulnerable to flames or embers, making these materials a better option than combustible wood if you cannot acquire or purchase the first four options.

Ideally, if it is combustible, the deck should be replaced with noncombustible materials such as metal or fiber cement. Most wood-plastic composites, along with higher-density tropical hardwood, and pressure impregnated fire retardant treated wood deck boards are less vulnerable to flames and embers.

Railings should be constructed of noncombustible materials, most importantly within the first 5 feet from the structure.

Particularly during fire season, clear the area underneath and around your deck to remove any combustible material, such as stored wood, barbeques, lawn mowers, flammable liquids, wood lawn furniture, furniture cushions, woody fibrous doormats, etc.

Flames spread to the home will be more likely if fine vegetative fuels (i.e. pine needles, leaf litter, and small twigs) have accumulated under or around the deck. Regularly sweep your deck and clear out any accumulated debris or leaf litter from underneath.

Avoid placement of any type of combustible mulch such as wood chips near the deck, and carefully select and maintain landscaping within 5 feet of a structure. If you have a deck, porch, or patio structure that sits above a graded surface, the area below should be devoid of vegetation and only noncombustible materials should be placed there (such as gravel).

Information about building permits is provided as a general guide. Permitting requirements are subject to change and property owners are responsible for ensuring their project meets all local requirements for development projects, including replacement, repairs, and retrofits. All retrofits must meet the requirements of the California Residential Code Section R337 and/or California Building Code Chapter 7A, where applicable, regardless of whether a permit is required or not.

In some cases, a building permit is required for deck repairs or replacement. Check with your local jurisdiction to determine specific permitting and inspection requirements for your project.

Platforms, walks, and wood decks not more than 30 inches above grade at any point and not over any basement or story below, not supporting any structure above, and not part of an accessible route, do not require a building permit. However, deck replacements or repairs in designated areas (County's Fire Hazard Severity Zones, High Fire Hazard Area / Wildland Urban Interface Areas) must meet the standards of the County's amended Building or Residential Code.
