Wind-blown embers are the principal cause of building ignition. Embers can easily enter attic vents, crawlspaces, eave vents, foundation vents, laundry vents, ductwork and other openings in your home leading to ignition from the inside of the building.

Although vents serve an important function for your home by removing moisture and adding oxygen, they must be configured with proper noncombustible metal screens (⅛” or smaller) to ensure that embers cannot enter. Laundry vents require different protection other than mesh, they are typically protected by vent guards that prevent lint buildup.

Installing the proper size and type of mesh screening can dramatically reduce ignition potential and limit the entry of embers into your home. This is one of the cheapest and easiest ways to improve your home’s vulnerability to ignition. Ember-resistant vents are addressed as part of the official Chapter 7A of the California Building Code.

On this page, Santa Barbara County Fire Safe Council recommends alterations that can be made to your structure to reduce its vulnerability to wildfire. At this time, we do not recommend or provide links to specific products or contractors.

The recommended alterations are ordered from least expensive to most expensive.

Most older vent coverings have ¼ -inch openings, which are vulnerable to ember intrusion. Replace ¼ -inch vents with ⅛ -inch or smaller vents to limit ember entry. Do not use fiberglass or plastic mesh because it can melt. There are generally two approaches:

  1. Purchase a roll of ⅛ -inch or smaller opening metal mesh (preferably galvanized steel). Simply install the smaller opening mesh over the pre-existing ¼ -inch. Placing the ⅛ -inch over the pre-existing ¼ -inch mesh has the added benefit of keeping rodents out while the ⅛ -inch mesh keeps embers out.
  2. Purchase a pre-fabricated vent screen with ⅛ -inch. Remove the existing fixture and insert the new fixture into the vent.

Attic, cathedral ceilings, and crawl spaces have vents. Adding metal mesh reduces the risk of intrusion in these areas. Heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) systems also have vents. For under-eave inlet vents, opt for a soffit (covered) eave design where possible. For outlet vents, opt for a ridge that is rated to resist wind-driven rain, with an external baffle. Turbine vents are also acceptable when a piece of ⅛ -inch mesh is attached to the bottom of the roof sheathing.

Additional types of vents that are found in homes include vents for household uses such as dryer vents, stove hood vents, and water heater vents. All of these vent openings should have noncombustible ⅛ -inch screening where appropriate to prevent ember entry. However, caution should be used to ensure enough airflow for dryer vents. Mesh installed to dryer vents requires regular cleaning to prevent the buildup of lint. All vents should be assessed on a case-by-case basis depending on what purpose they serve in your home.

The recommended maintenance options are listed from easiest to hardest to complete.

Small mesh vents can accumulate debris such as leaf litter and spiderwebs which can impact the air flow. To ensure your home is receiving adequate airflow, make sure to keep vents clean. Annually inspect your vents for wear and tear. Over time the vents on your home will degrade due to natural wear and tear from weather and wildlife. When a vent weakens or has holes in it the structure becomes more susceptible to ember intrusion.

Seal any gaps and cracks with a sealant like caulking. Replace any damaged vents or install ⅛ -inch or smaller mesh over the damaged areas.

Take an inventory of what you have stored in your attic and crawl spaces and remove all combustible materials like cardboard boxes or clothing. If embers enter, this can reduce the potential for stored items to ignite the rest of the home.
