Having up-to-code windows and skylights will help protect your home.

Heat from a wildfire can cause windows to burst and break even before the home is on fire, which allows embers to enter and start fires inside the home. Single-paned and large windows are particularly vulnerable, as well as plastic-domed skylights. Combustible framing materials can ignite or deform, and the glass can crack and fall out due to thermal stress from direct flame contact or radiant heat, allowing fire to enter your home.

On this page, Santa Barbara County Fire Safe Council recommends alterations that can be made to your structure to reduce its vulnerability to wildfire. At this time, we do not recommend or provide links to specific products or contractors.

The recommended alterations are ordered from least expensive to most expensive.

Remove combustible vegetation below your windows and replace combustible mulch with a noncombustible material such as gravel, river rock, or brick. If areas directly beneath your windows are combustible, it will increase the risk of your windows breaking or bursting. Learn more about how to make the area in front of your windows more fire-resistant here.

Installing fine-mesh 1/16 -inch metal window screens also helps protect your home from ember ignition. Both plastic-clad fiberglass and metal screening will reduce radiant exposure to the glass and protect against ember entry, but neither will protect against flames. This is an important step in addition to making sure you have double-pane windows. Vinyl screens can melt and possibly ignite and should be replaced.

Plastic skylights should be replaced, especially if you have vegetation overhanging your roof even if it is trimmed 10ft away, because the plastic can melt due to heat exposure from a fire.

The type of skylight that is best depends on the slope of your roof, but in all cases, dual-pane, tempered glass is safest.

  • - For steep-sloped roofs, flat dual-pane skylights offer the best protection against ember or fire ignition. If you can add a noncombustible screen to any skylight, it will offer additional protection.
  • - For low-sloped or flat roofs, domed skylights are preferred to limit the amount of debris accumulation.

Dual-pane windows have two sheets of glass separated by airspace and also have the benefits of energy conservation and insulation. Single-pane windows are more common in older structures and are highly vulnerable to bursting or breaking from the heat of a wildfire.

If you are unable to replace all single-pane windows around your home, consider starting with windows on the first floor, particularly those that face steep slopes or have vegetation adjacent to or near the window. These windows are the most susceptible to heat from fire and most likely to break.

If you are adding new windows or constructing a home, be aware of your local conditions and consider limiting the size and number of windows that face steep slopes, large areas of vegetation or the prevailing wind direction, where a wildfire would most likely be coming from.

The recommended maintenance options are listed from easiest to hardest to complete.

When you leave your house for vacation or even to run to the store during high fire season, remember to close your windows and skylights in case a fire starts while you are away. Make sure all windows are fully closed before evacuating. In the event of a fire, an open window will allow embers to easily enter the home.

Both domed and flat skylights can be vulnerable points on your home from extended radiant heat exposure, and each uses a metal flashing to protect the framing. It is critical that the flashing is maintained and all seals are intact to avoid risks for all types of skylights.

Regularly clear any vegetative debris around them, which can cause ignition.

In almost all cases, a building permit is required for window and skylight repairs or replacement. Check with your local jurisdiction to determine specific permitting and inspection requirements for your project.

Information about building permits is provided as a general guide. Permitting requirements are subject to change and property owners are responsible for ensuring their project meets all local requirements for development projects, including replacement, repairs, and retrofits. All retrofits must meet the requirements of the California Residential Code Section R337 and/or California Building Code Chapter 7A, where applicable, regardless of whether a permit is required or not.

Window replacements require a building permit. In some cases, they may also require a planning permit. For more information on building permits for window or skylight projects in the County of Santa Barbara, visit:  https://www.countyofsb.org/1001/Building-Code The County of Santa Barbara recommends that you contact planning and building staff for specific direction on ANY retrofit project.

All window and exterior door replacements require a building permit. This includes "retrofit windows" and "new construction flanged windows." All window permits will require a floor plan, site plan, and a window schematic to accompany the application. For more information on building permits for window or skylight projects in the City of Santa Barbara, visit:  https://santabarbaraca.gov/government/departments/community-development/building-safety
