Properly maintaining gutters is an important component of home hardening.

Gutters can be a conduit for fire to reach your roof, eaves, siding, and attic if they are constructed out of combustible materials and/or contain debris. Wind-blown debris that often collects in gutters (including leaves and pine needles from nearby and overhanging trees) can be easily ignited by embers.

If ignited, combustible debris in the gutter will expose the edge of the roof covering. Gutters and downspouts should be constructed of noncombustible materials such as galvanized steel, copper, or aluminum and are ideally covered with metal screening (or gutter guards) to prevent leaf litter from accumulating.

On this page, Santa Barbara County Fire Safe Council recommends alterations that can be made to your structure to reduce its vulnerability to wildfire. At this time, we do not recommend or provide links to specific products or contractors.

The recommended alterations are ordered from least expensive to most expensive.

Metal gutter covers (also known as gutter guards) can be installed over or in your gutters to reduce the amount of vegetative debris that accumulates in your gutter. Types of leaf guards include metal mesh screens and metal hoods that fit into the gutter.

Some types of leaf guards do not prevent all types of debris from accumulating in the gutter (e.g. mesh-type leaf guards allow pine needles to accumulate). Check regularly and remove debris.

Note, gutter guards and covers are not essential to the safety of your home. However, they help reduce the amount of maintenance needed to keep the gutter clear of debris.

Vinyl or plastic gutters easily melt and can fall to the ground. Debris and combustible gutters will continue to burn and ignite nearby flammable material such as mulch, vegetation, wood piles, and wood siding within Zone 0 (first 0-5 feet from the structure). A burning gutter can also damage the roof which will then make it susceptible to embers.

Replace vinyl or plastic gutters with noncombustible metal gutters, such as galvanized steel, copper, or aluminum. You can tell if your gutters are vinyl or plastic if they sound hollow when tapped.

Some metal gutters have an integral flashing piece that serves the function of a stand-alone drip edge. If a drip edge is not present, install one to help protect the roof edge (sheathing and fascia) from flame exposure and minimize the entry of embers into eaves.

Embers will ignite accumulated debris in any gutter. Regardless of the gutter material, maintain your gutters to ensure they are free of combustible debris. Dry leaves and other debris that collect in your rain gutters can be dangerous and ignite very easily from wind-blown embers during wildfires. Cleaning your rain gutters, even if you have gutter covers, regularly can prevent this from being a threat.
