Your siding can make a difference in protecting your home from wildfire.

Siding - what the external walls of your home are covered with - can either protect your home or make your home more exposed to fire. If your home has combustible siding, it can ignite or melt and become a conduit for flames to enter vulnerable parts of your home, such as windows or the under-eave area, and stud cavities (then potentially entering into your attic). If the siding is ignited, it can move flames and/or embers to the horizontal or vertical lap joints of your home and allow flames to enter.

Combustible siding can also be ignited by other building components, such as decks, fences, vegetation, or radiant heat from another structure (within 30 feet of your home). It is best to have noncombustible siding on your home, such as stone, stucco, Hardie board, metal (no aluminum), concrete, or brick. However, if that isn’t feasible, there are several things you can do to protect your home if you have combustible siding.

On this page, Santa Barbara County Fire Safe Council recommends alterations that can be made to your structure to reduce its vulnerability to wildfire. At this time, we do not recommend or provide links to specific products or contractors.

The recommended alterations are ordered from least expensive to most expensive.

If you choose not to replace combustible siding, the use of metal flashing and fire-retardant gypsum board at its base can help to reduce ignition potential. Six -inches or more of vertical noncombustible material should be maintained between the ground and the start of the siding as this area accumulates the most embers.

While this major upgrade to your home is costly, it is a long-term investment to protect your home against wildfire. Noncombustible siding on your home, such as stone, stucco, metal, concrete, or brick are optimal choices. In particular, if you have older wooden siding, this is an important consideration.

The recommended maintenance options are listed from easiest to hardest to complete.

Annually inspect your siding for any cracks or gaps, especially where two sides of the structure meet (corner/edge). If there are many cracks in your siding, hire a painting contractor to “prime” the exterior as if they will paint it. A good contractor will meticulously seal up all small gaps and color-match the caulk with the color of the siding.

Inspect the bottom of door frames as these often have wear and tear over time, leading to small gaps that will enable embers to get inside the structure. This will cause the home to burn from the inside out.

Some types of siding, such as concrete or stucco, have a base plate (aka weep screed) between the siding and the foundation. Over time this plate may become exposed, leaving a gap for embers to get inside of the siding and burn the house from the inside out. Annually inspect this base plate using your fingers. If the plate feels flush with the siding, then it is not a vulnerability. If you can see cracks or feel a minor gap between the plate and the siding, patch with the appropriate material depending on your construction (ie: caulking, stucco, cement) or call a reputable contractor to have it repaired.

Incorporating a noncombustible or low-combustible zone within 0-5 feet next to your home will reduce the vulnerability of your siding to ignition. Please click here to learn about creating a fire safe zone against your home.
