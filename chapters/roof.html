Having a proper up to code roof is a critical step in protecting your home from wildfire

Why are roofs important?

If you can afford it and you don’t currently have a Class A roof, it is the most important home hardening upgrade you can do and should be a top priority. Homes ignite in one of three ways: embers/firebrands, radiant heat exposure or direct flame contact. Wind-blown embers are responsible for the majority of building ignitions, and roofs are a critical part of protecting your home.

Woodshake roofs and other roofs that are not Class A are vulnerable to wind-blown ember ignition. A Class A roof in good condition is the most important home hardening upgrade you can make to protect your house from wildfire and should be a top priority.

Common stand-alone Class A roof coverings include clay tiles, slate, asphalt fiberglass composition shingles, concrete and flat/barrel-shaped tiles, and some metal roofing materials. Common assembly-rated Class A roof coverings include aluminum roofs (because of low melting point), pressure-impregnated fire-retardant treated shake or shingle covering (not allowed in many jurisdictions), and some recycled rubber and or plastic composite materials.

On this page, Santa Barbara County Fire Safe Council recommends alterations that can be made to your structure to reduce its vulnerability to wildfire. At this time, we do not recommend or provide links to specific products or contractors.

The recommended alterations are ordered from least expensive to most expensive.

Embers and firebrands can enter your home through your chimney or stovepipe outlet if it is not protected and maintained correctly. Embers can also escape from your chimney and start a fire in nearby trees or other vegetation. Cover your chimney and stove pipe outlets with metal spark arrestors that are securely attached and made of welded or woven wire ⅛ -inch mesh screens. Install metal-flashing at roof-to-siding intersections to reduce the vulnerability of the chimney.

Fill in openings at roof edges with sealed bird stops or cement to reduce ember ignition potential. This is most common with clay tile and metal roofs. Check for gaps between your roof covering and sheathing and seal those too.

Debris and leaf litter can build up at the intersecting joints of the roof. Installing metal flashing where the roof intersects with the structure and at intersecting joints can protect these vulnerable areas.

Roof covering fire ratings are Class A, B, C, or unrated; with Class A providing the best performance and now required by Code when replacing your roof. Common Class A roof coverings include asphalt fiberglass composition shingles, concrete, or flat/barrel-shaped tiles.

Consult with a reputable contractor if you are unsure about your roof’s rating and which roofing material is appropriate for your structure.

The recommended maintenance options are listed from easiest to hardest to complete.

Materials wear and tear over time, so it is essential to annually inspect your roof and eaves for any gaps, cracks, or broken tiles where embers can get in. Learn more about Eaves and Soffits here.

Regularly keep the roof and gutter clear of debris and leaf litter, especially in intersecting joints where debris generally accumulates. Learn more about how to make your gutters less vulnerable to ignition here.

Skylights and solar panels can increase the vulnerability of your roof by accumulating flammable debris and leaf litter. Please click here to learn about protecting your roof from vulnerabilities caused by skylights.

Trees should be trimmed at least 10 feet away from chimneys to prevent the spread of embers from your fireplace to the tree.

It is also recommended to remove tree limbs overhanging the roof. However, if you choose to keep tree branches that overhang the roof then maintain a 10-foot vertical gap between the roof and overhanging branches.

Many homes in the area have Coast Live Oaks with limbs overhanging roofs. These trees are protected and, depending on where you live, have different restrictions for alterations, especially for large, well-established trees. Studies have shown that they provide shading from embers and removing large limbs can shift the weight distribution of the tree so removing them isn’t recommended. If you do want to pursue the removal of limbs, refer to your local Community Plan by following this link and contacting a trained Arborist.

Information about building permits is provided as a general guide. Permitting requirements are subject to change and property owners are responsible for ensuring their project meets all local requirements for development projects, including replacement, repairs, and retrofits. All retrofits must meet the requirements of the California Residential Code Section R337 and/or California Building Code Chapter 7A, where applicable, regardless of whether a permit is required or not.

In most cases, a building permit is required for roof repairs or replacement. Check with your local jurisdiction to determine specific permitting and inspection requirements for your project.

Roof replacement or repair projects are exempt from a permit if the project is less than 100 square feet of finished material within one calendar year. Roof replacements or repairs in designated areas (County's Fire Hazard Severity Zones, High Fire Hazard Area / Wildland Urban Interface Areas) must meet roof assembly and construction standards of the County's amended Building or Residential Code, unless they are exempted, such as alterations to the existing buildings where less than 50% of roof framing is modified or replaced. For more information on building permits for roof repair or replacement projects in the County of Santa Barbara, visit: https://www.countyofsb.org/1001/Building-Code The County of Santa Barbara recommends that you contact planning and building staff for specific directions on ANY retrofit project.

For more information on building permits for roof repair or replacement projects in the City of Santa Barbara, visit:  https://santabarbaraca.gov/government/departments/community-development/building-safety

For more information on building permits for roof repair or replacement projects in the City of Carpinteria, visit:  https://carpinteriaca.gov/city-hall/community-development/building-and-safety/

The entire roof covering of every existing structure where more than 50 percent of the total roof area is replaced within any one-year period, the entire roof covering of every new structure, and any roof covering applied in the alteration, repair, or replacement of the roof of any existing structure, shall be a fire-retardant roof covering that is at least Class A. For more information on building permits for roof repair or replacement projects in the City of Goleta, visit:  https://www.cityofgoleta.org/your-city/planning-and-environmental-review

Replacement of existing and/or application of additional new roof material which amounts to more than 10% of the total roof area will require removal and replacement of the entire roof with conforming Class A roofing material. The Montecito Fire Protection District reviews plans and/or roofing contracts to verify that a conforming “Class A” roof is being proposed for installation. The contractor or owner’s agent is required to produce such documentation for review by Fire District personnel. For more information on requirements for roof repair or replacement projects in the Town of Montecito, visit:  https://www.montecitofire.com/development-standards
