Heat from a wildfire can cause windows to burst and break even before the home is on fire, which allows embers to enter and start fires inside the home. Single-paned and large windows are particularly vulnerable, as well as plastic-domed skylights. Combustible framing materials can ignite or deform, and the glass can crack and fall out due to thermal stress from direct flame contact or radiant heat and let fire enter.

Replace your old windows with dual-pane, tempered glass: Confirm that you have dual-paned windows (with one pane of tempered glass) in order to protect your home. Dual pane windows have two sheets of glass separated by airspace, and also have the benefits of energy conservation and insulation. Single-pane windows are more common in older structures, and are highly vulnerable to bursting or breaking just from the heat of a wildfire.

Also, if you are adding new windows or constructing a home, be aware of your local conditions and consider limiting the size and number of windows that face large areas of vegetation or the prevailing wind direction, where a wildfire would most likely be coming from. When you leave your house, close your windows and skylights, otherwise embers can enter through openings.

Installing fine-mesh 1/16” metal window screens also helps protect your home from ember ignition. Both plastic-clad fiberglass and metal screening will reduce radiant exposure to the glass and protect against ember entry, but neither will protect against flames. This is an important step in addition to making sure you have double-paned windows. Vinyl screens can melt and possibly ignite, and thus should be replaced.

Do not plant combustible vegetation below your windows or use combustible mulch. If areas directly beneath your windows are combustible, it will increase the risk of your windows breaking or bursting.

Regularly clear any vegetative debris around them, which can cause ignition. If your skylights are older, they may need to be switched out, especially if they are the combustible plastic domed type, which can melt due to heat from a fire and allow embers to enter. The type of skylight that is best depends on the slope of your roof, but in all cases, dual pane, tempered glass is safest.

For steep-sloped roofs, flat dual pane skylights offer the best protection against embers or fire ignition. If you can add a noncombustible screen to any skylight, it will offer additional protection.

If you have a low-slope or flat roof, domed skylights are preferred to limit the amount of debris accumulation. Skylights on any roof are still vulnerable to extended radiant heat exposure from nearby vegetation and combustible materials. Make sure to close skylights that open when you are away.

Both domed and flat skylights can be vulnerable points on your home from extended radiant heat exposure, and each uses a metal flashing to protect the framing. It is critical that the flashing is maintained and all seals are intact to avoid risks for ALL types of skylights.

In almost all cases, a building permit is required for window and skylight repairs or replacement. Check with your local jurisdiction to determine specific permitting and inspection requirements for your project.

Information pertaining to building permits is provided as a general guide. Permitting requirements are subject to change and property owners are responsible for ensuring their project meets all local requirements for development projects, including replacement, repairs, and retrofits. All retrofits must meet the requirements of the California Residential Code Section R337 and/or California Building Code Chapter 7A, where applicable, regardless of whether a permit is required or not.

Window replacements require a building permit. In some cases, they may also require a planning permit. For more information on building permits for window or skylight projects in the County of Santa Barbara, visit:  https://www.countyofsb.org/1001/Building-Code The County of Santa Barbara recommends that you contact planning and building staff for specific direction on ANY retrofit project.

All window and exterior door replacements require a building permit. This includes "retrofit windows" and "new construction flanged windows." All window permits will require a floor plan, site plan, and a window schematic to accompany the application. For more information on building permits for window or skylight projects in the City of Santa Barbara, visit:  https://santabarbaraca.gov/government/departments/community-development/building-safety

What is the Wildfire Preparedness Evaluation?

A Wildfire Preparedness Evaluation is a free and voluntary review of the vulnerability of your house and landscape to wildfire and ember-ignition. During this assessment, an evaluator will:

  • review defensible space and home hardening principles
  • make specific recommendations based on your property
  • review your evacuation preparedness so you can be safer and feel more prepared

For your convenience, we have provided three ways for you to receive an evaluation:

  • Complete the evaluation entirely by yourself
  • Request an in-person expert to evaluate your property
  • Hybrid option – Complete as much of the evaluation as possible by yourself and a SBFSC staff member can finish the report in person
